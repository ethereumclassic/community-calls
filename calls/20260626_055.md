### Philosophy Chat: How Does Value Accrue to the ETC Token?

Setting aside how useful the network is, what gives the ETC token itself its value? One argument from the community: on-chain activity nets to zero at the margin, since a user buys ETC to pay gas and a miner sells roughly the same amount to cover electricity, so more transactions need not move the price. If that holds, the token's value looks less like a claim on network activity and more like a monetary premium or Schelling point, the way Bitcoin and gold are valued because others will accept them. Is that the right way to see it, and if so, what actually drives the premium?

### Futarchy: Governing by Prediction Markets

A quick primer to kick things off. Futarchy is a governance model from economist Robin Hanson, summed up as "vote on values, but bet on beliefs" ([original write-up](https://mason.gmu.edu/~rhanson/futarchy.html)). Elected representatives decide what to optimize for by defining a measure of welfare, and everything else is left to betting markets. For each proposal, conditional prediction markets estimate whether enacting it would raise that welfare measure, and the rule is roughly: when the market clearly says a proposal would improve expected welfare, it becomes policy. The pitch is that markets aggregate dispersed information and reward people who actually know something, while staying neutral on values. The usual objections are that the welfare metric is hard to define and measure after the fact, and that thin or manipulable markets may not predict reliably.

Tying it to the recurring question of where ETC decisions should happen:

- Could decision markets help break the ECIP and protocol-direction deadlock, or would the fight just move to defining the metric?
- What would ETC's welfare measure even be: price, hashrate, usage, or something else?
- On a small chain, are prediction markets too thin and too easy to manipulate to trust?
- Is there a lighter-weight version worth trying, such as non-binding markets that simply inform a call like this one?
